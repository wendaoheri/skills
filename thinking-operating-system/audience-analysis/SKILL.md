# Audience Analysis

## Overview

Understanding your audience is the foundation of any effective training
programme. A thorough audience analysis collects information about who
learners are – their age, education, job role and experience – as well as
what they know, how they feel and what constraints they face. According
to training design guidance, effective audience analysis should cover
demographics, subject‑matter expertise, attitudes toward the topic,
tools and systems used, language and culture, environment, prior
training and communication preferences【122398275456329†L347-L410】.  The
results inform every aspect of the training programme and are essential
for changing behaviour【122398275456329†L347-L410】.

## When to Use

Use this skill:

- At the very beginning of a training project to ensure that the course
  content, format and tone are appropriate for the intended learners.
- When tailoring existing material to a new audience or demographic.
- Before choosing delivery methods (e.g. in‑person, virtual, self‑paced)
  or deciding on examples and case studies.
- Whenever you need to segment learners into groups (e.g. novices vs.
  experts) and design differentiated experiences.

## Process

Follow these steps to build a useful audience profile:

### Step 1 – Gather demographic and role information

1. Collect basic demographics: age ranges, education levels, years of
   experience and job titles. These factors influence prior knowledge,
   preferred tone and pace.
2. Identify organisational role and seniority to understand decision‑making
   authority and context (e.g. frontline staff vs. managers).

### Step 2 – Assess knowledge and attitudes

1. Determine subject‑matter expertise: are learners complete novices,
   intermediate, or experts? This affects the depth of explanation
   required.
2. Note existing attitudes toward the topic (enthusiastic, sceptical,
   anxious, resistant) to anticipate engagement challenges【122398275456329†L347-L410】.
3. Investigate any misconceptions or myths that may need addressing.

### Step 3 – Identify tools, systems and language

1. Record the tools and systems learners use in their job (software,
   hardware, processes). Training examples should mirror these tools so
   learners can apply new skills immediately【122398275456329†L347-L410】.
2. Note preferred or required language(s) and any cultural considerations
   (e.g. local idioms, formality, diversity needs).

### Step 4 – Understand environment and constraints

1. Determine whether participants are remote, hybrid or on‑site; note
   connectivity issues, time zones and workspace limitations.
2. Understand time constraints: how long can learners be away from their
   jobs? Are there peak periods when training should be avoided?
3. Note any accessibility requirements (e.g. screen readers, closed
   captions).

### Step 5 – Review prior training and communication preferences

1. Ask about past training experiences: what formats were effective?
   Which ones were disliked? This informs modality choices.
2. Determine how learners prefer to receive information (email, chat,
   printed manuals, videos) so follow‑ups and materials can be delivered
   effectively.

### Step 6 – Segment and summarise

1. Group participants into segments with shared characteristics (e.g.
   junior analysts, seasoned managers). Summarise each segment’s
   demographics, knowledge level, attitudes and constraints.
2. For each segment, list implications for training design (e.g.
   beginner‑friendly explanations, advanced case studies, supportive
   mentoring).
3. Validate your findings with stakeholders or a sample of learners to
   confirm accuracy.

## Output Format

Your audience analysis should result in an **Audience Profile Summary**
containing:

1. **Segments:** names or descriptions of learner groups.
2. **Characteristics:** key demographics, roles, experience and attitudes.
3. **Knowledge Level:** description of existing knowledge or skills.
4. **Tools & Environment:** systems used, language, cultural notes,
   environment and accessibility considerations.
5. **Training Needs:** gaps or misconceptions to address.
6. **Design Implications:** specific recommendations for content depth,
   tone, examples, pacing and modality for each segment.

Present the summary as a table or bullet list. Keep detailed raw data
outside of the report for privacy. Use the findings to shape
learning‑objective design, storytelling and case selection.

## Example

**Scenario:** You are designing an AI productivity workshop for a
multinational sales team.

**Audience Profile Summary:**

- **Segment:** Junior Sales Associates (20–30 years old, 0–3 years
  experience). **Characteristics:** Comfortable with technology but have
  little domain knowledge; enthusiastic about AI but unclear about
  limitations. **Tools & Environment:** Use CRM and cloud office suite;
  primarily remote. **Training Needs:** Basic understanding of AI
  capabilities, hands‑on demos. **Design Implications:** Keep language
  simple, include live demos and short exercises; schedule sessions in
  small blocks to accommodate sales calls.
- **Segment:** Regional Managers (35–50 years old, 8–15 years
  experience). **Characteristics:** Experienced in sales strategy;
  sceptical about new tools; concerned about ROI. **Tools &
  Environment:** Work hybrid; use advanced analytics dashboards. **Training
  Needs:** Strategic applications of AI; case studies showing business
  impact. **Design Implications:** Provide data‑driven examples,
  emphasise ROI and change management; allocate time for discussion.

## Notes

- Audience analysis is not a one‑off task. Update the profile as
  organisational roles or technologies change.
- Respect privacy when collecting personal data; aggregate results
  whenever possible.
- Use the analysis to adapt examples, tone and pacing; do not assume
  learners share your background.
