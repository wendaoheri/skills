# Case Demo Builder

## Overview

Case studies are powerful tools for anchoring theory in concrete
decisions. They bring abstract ideas to life, expose hidden
constraints and create a safe space for debate【700260952801499†L207-L216】.
Unlike lectures, case discussions focus on trade‑offs rather than
“right answers,” encouraging learners to practise reasoning and
judgement【700260952801499†L207-L216】. Effective case‑based training
requires careful selection of cases, clear instructions, facilitation
to prevent drift and structured debriefs that emphasise application.

## When to Use

Use this skill:

- After introducing theoretical frameworks, to let learners practise
  applying concepts.
- When the goal is to develop decision‑making, critical thinking or
  problem‑scoping skills.
- To surface real‑world constraints and trade‑offs in areas such as
  strategy, product design or ethics.
- When learners benefit from hearing diverse viewpoints and engaging in
  safe debate.

## Process

Follow these steps to build a case demonstration that sparks learning:

### Step 1 – Define objectives and choose a relevant case

1. Clarify the learning objectives: which competencies should the case
   develop? Use the results of `learning-objective-designer` to
   anchor your selection.
2. Select or develop a case that mirrors the learners’ context and
   highlights the intended trade‑offs【700260952801499†L207-L216】. Ensure
   complexity matches learners’ expertise and that key variables are
   clearly presented.

### Step 2 – Write a concise scenario and task

1. Draft the case background including essential facts, constraints and
   stakeholder perspectives. Avoid excessive backstory that distracts
   from the decision【700260952801499†L221-L283】.
2. State the task succinctly: what should learners do (e.g. analyse
   options, choose a course of action, identify assumptions)? Set a
   time limit to focus discussion【700260952801499†L221-L283】.
3. Provide any supporting data or exhibits needed to make an informed
   decision.

### Step 3 – Select a facilitation method

Choose one or more of the following approaches【700260952801499†L221-L283】:

1. **Rewrite the outcome:** Change one variable (e.g. budget, timeline,
   market condition) and ask learners to predict ripple effects.
2. **Peer interviews:** Ask learners to interview each other about
   assumptions, unearthing biases and hidden information.
3. **Storyboard the case:** Have participants map the narrative
   structure (context → trigger → decision → action → result) to
   visualise causality and identify decision points.
4. **Mini‑case creation:** Encourage learners to write brief cases from
   their own experience, then discuss trade‑offs.

### Step 4 – Prepare discussion questions and guidance

1. Frame open‑ended questions focusing on trade‑offs, constraints and
   alternative paths rather than on “correct” answers【700260952801499†L207-L216】.
2. Include prompts to uncover assumptions, evaluate evidence quality and
   consider stakeholder impacts.
3. Develop a simple scoring or feedback guide if decisions will be
   presented; emphasise reasoning and completeness over presentation
   style【700260952801499†L221-L283】.

### Step 5 – Conduct the session

1. Form small groups or pairs and explain the task, timing and output
   format (e.g. short presentation, written summary).
2. Monitor discussions, intervening only to clarify tasks or redirect
   off‑topic debates. Prevent one or two voices from dominating and
   encourage quieter participants to contribute【700260952801499†L221-L283】.
3. Keep share‑outs concise (e.g. two minutes per group) to maintain
   energy and allow time for debrief【700260952801499†L221-L283】.

### Step 6 – Debrief and connect to job application

1. Summarise the different decisions and reasoning presented. Highlight
   how changing variables or assumptions led to different outcomes.
2. Reinforce key concepts and connect them back to learning objectives.
3. Ask learners to articulate how they would apply the insights to
   their own work; provide a concrete job‑use example to close the
   loop【700260952801499†L221-L283】.

## Output Format

Create a **Case Pack** containing:

1. **Case Title & Context:** A brief title and short paragraph setting
   the scene.
2. **Learning Objectives:** The specific competencies the case is
   designed to develop.
3. **Scenario:** The facts, constraints and stakeholders involved.
4. **Task:** Clear instructions including deliverable and time limit.
5. **Method:** Which facilitation approach(es) you will use (rewrite
   outcome, peer interviews, storyboard, mini‑case).
6. **Discussion Questions:** A list of prompts focusing on trade‑offs,
   assumptions and stakeholder impacts.
7. **Facilitation & Debrief Notes:** Tips for managing the session,
   pitfalls to avoid and key points to reinforce in the debrief.

Present the pack as a structured document or table. Keep the scenario
concise and focus on the decision at hand. The debrief notes should
include how the case links back to learners’ jobs.

## Example

**Case Title & Context:** “AI Ethics in Client Recommendations” – A
wealth management firm is deciding whether to deploy an AI tool that
recommends investment products to clients.

**Learning Objectives:** Assess ethical and regulatory risks of AI; weigh
short‑term business gains against long‑term client trust.

**Scenario:** The firm’s marketing team wants to use an AI model to
suggest products based on client data. Compliance warns of potential
mis‑selling. Market share is falling, and competitors have adopted
similar tools. Regulations require transparency and suitability.

**Task:** In small groups, decide whether to proceed with the AI tool,
modify the proposal or reject it. You have 20 minutes to prepare a
recommendation and rationale.

**Method:** Storyboard the case to identify where ethical risks emerge.

**Discussion Questions:**
1. What assumptions are we making about clients’ understanding of AI?
2. How do transparency requirements affect our decision?
3. What trade‑offs exist between market share and client trust?
4. How might modifying the tool’s design mitigate risks?

**Facilitation & Debrief Notes:** Encourage groups to consider legal
obligations and reputational impact. Keep presentations to two minutes.
Summarise common themes and ask each group how they would explain their
decision to a client.

## Notes

- Choose cases that mirror learners’ environment and avoid exotic
  scenarios; relevance increases engagement【700260952801499†L221-L283】.
- State tasks succinctly and specify timing; vague instructions lead to
  confusion【700260952801499†L221-L283】.
- Use a simple scoring guide if needed, but prioritise reasoning and
  trade‑off analysis over presentation polish【700260952801499†L221-L283】.
- Beware of pitfalls: too much backstory, debate drift, evaluating
  superficial presentation skills and allowing dominating voices to
  monopolise the discussion【700260952801499†L221-L283】.
