# Training Storyline Builder

## Overview

Human brains are wired for stories, not bullet lists. Traditional
corporate training often dumps information on learners and treats them as
cogs in a process, leading to disengagement【632112647987370†L26-L105】. In
contrast, a narrative framework frames the learner as the hero on a
journey, guided by a mentor through challenges toward a transformation
【632112647987370†L26-L105】. Using the hero’s journey and narrative
structures helps learners see why the training matters and remember what
they learn. The “training triforce” – combining asynchronous
resources, live events and documentation – supports this journey by
providing just‑in‑time support【632112647987370†L26-L105】.

## When to Use

Use this skill when designing:

- Course outlines for workshops, webinars, boot camps or self‑paced
  courses where sustained engagement is critical.
- Training sessions that need to connect disparate topics into a
  cohesive whole.
- Onboarding programmes where learners must progress from novice to
  competent practitioner.
- Any educational experience where you want to motivate learners by
  showing them as protagonists rather than passive recipients.

## Process

Design your training narrative in the following stages:

### Step 1 – Define the protagonist and baseline

1. Identify who the learners are (see your audience analysis) and
   describe their “ordinary world” – the current state or problem
   before training. Clarify what gaps or frustrations they face.
2. State the desired transformation (e.g. “become confident AI
   assistants” or “deliver data‑driven reports”). This becomes the
   learner’s goal.

### Step 2 – Articulate the call to adventure

1. Describe the change, challenge or opportunity that prompts
   training【632112647987370†L26-L105】. This could be a new technology,
   market pressure or organisational strategy.
2. Show why maintaining the status quo is no longer acceptable to
   create urgency and relevance.

### Step 3 – Introduce the mentor and resources

1. Establish the trainer, coach or AI assistant as the “mentor” who
   guides the learner. List tools, templates or platforms available to
   support the journey.
2. Align these resources with the learner’s knowledge gaps and
   objectives.

### Step 4 – Map the journey into stages

1. Use the hero’s journey stages to organise your course:
   - **Crossing the Threshold:** learners commit to training. Provide
     orientation, set expectations and establish psychological safety.
   - **Tests, Allies and Enemies:** learners face progressively
     challenging activities (quizzes, simulations, case studies) and
     receive feedback. Encourage collaboration and highlight common
     pitfalls.【632112647987370†L26-L105】
   - **Ordeal:** introduce a capstone project or complex problem that
     integrates multiple skills. This is the most challenging part of
     training.
   - **Reward and Return:** learners reflect on what they have learned,
     celebrate achievements and plan how to apply the “elixir” of new
     knowledge back in their work environment【632112647987370†L26-L105】.
2. For each stage, align specific learning objectives, activities and
   modalities (e.g. asynchronous readings before live practice, group
   breakout discussions during tests, job aids for the return).

### Step 5 – Blend modalities using the training triforce

1. Combine asynchronous resources (videos, readings, quizzes), live
   events (workshops, webinars, coaching sessions) and documentation
   (checklists, templates) to support each stage【632112647987370†L26-L105】.
2. Ensure that asynchronous materials prepare learners for live sessions
   and that documentation serves as a reference during and after the
   journey.

### Step 6 – Weave storytelling elements

1. Craft compelling openings that draw learners into the narrative;
   use analogies, real‑world anecdotes or surprising statistics.
2. Introduce conflict and resolution: present problems to solve or
   obstacles to overcome, then reveal solutions and insights.
3. Use characters (personas) and scenarios that mirror learners’ real
   challenges to foster empathy.
4. Maintain a consistent theme or motif throughout (e.g. “heroic
   explorer,” “detective” or “architect”) to tie elements together.

### Step 7 – Conclude with return and application

1. Guide learners to articulate how they will apply their new skills in
   their work (the “return with the elixir”).
2. Encourage them to set personal goals or commitments and provide
   follow‑up resources or communities of practice.
3. Close the story by revisiting the initial call to adventure and
   showing how the learner has transformed.

## Output Format

Produce a **Storyline Outline** table or bullet list with the following
columns/sections:

1. **Stage:** Name of the narrative stage (e.g. Ordinary World,
   Call to Adventure, Tests, Ordeal, Reward/Return).
2. **Purpose:** How this stage supports the learner’s journey and
   learning objectives.
3. **Content & Activities:** Summary of topics, exercises and
   interactions planned.
4. **Modality:** Delivery format(s) used (asynchronous, live event,
   documentation) and timing relative to other stages.

Ensure the outline flows logically from one stage to the next and
explicitly links back to the learning objectives.

## Example

**Training:** AI office productivity course for analysts.

**Storyline Outline:**

- **Ordinary World / Protagonist:** Analysts manually compile data for
  hours and feel overwhelmed by information overload. *Purpose:* set
  baseline and empathise.
- **Call to Adventure:** Market volatility and time pressure require
  faster insights; management introduces AI tools. *Purpose:* create
  urgency and relevance.
- **Meeting the Mentor:** Trainer introduces the AI assistant, shows
  basic features and provides cheat sheets. *Modality:* short
  asynchronous video + live Q&A.
- **Crossing the Threshold:** Learners commit to try AI tools on a
  guided exercise; expectations and ground rules are set. *Modality:*
  live workshop.
- **Tests and Allies:** Learners practise summarising reports and
  generating briefs; they collaborate in breakout groups and discuss
  challenges. *Modality:* live breakout session + collaborative
  document.
- **Ordeal:** Each learner completes a mini project using AI to analyse
  a complex dataset under time pressure. *Purpose:* integrate skills.
- **Reward and Return:** Learners present their findings, reflect on
  lessons, identify next steps and plan how to use AI in their jobs.
  *Modality:* live presentations + follow‑up job aids.

## Notes

- Keep the narrative authentic; avoid contrived stories that patronise
  learners. Use real data and contexts whenever possible.
- Adapt the hero’s journey stages flexibly; not every training needs
  every stage, but progression and transformation should be evident.
- Tie storytelling to concrete learning outcomes; a good story without
  instructional substance can entertain but not educate.
